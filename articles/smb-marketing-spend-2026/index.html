What Should a Small Business
Actually Spend on Marketing?

The SBA says 7–8% of revenue. Most small businesses spend $534 a month. That gap is exactly why most small businesses stay stuck. Here's how to find your real number — and where to put every dollar.

The Quick Answer

Most established small businesses should invest 7–10% of annual revenue in marketing. B2C businesses lean toward the top of that range — or higher. B2B businesses sit a bit lower. And if you're actively trying to grow, not just maintain, plan for 10–14%.

Sources: U.S. SBA guideline, Gartner CMO Survey 2025, CMO Survey (Deloitte / Duke / AMA)

What you lose when you underinvest

It's not just missed growth. Underspending on marketing has compounding costs that show up in ways that feel unrelated.

  • You become invisible to new customers

    If people can't find you — on Google, social, or AI search — they find your competitor instead. Every month without marketing presence is a month of lost discovery.

  • Competitors compound their lead

    Every dollar a competitor invests in SEO, ads, or content is building an asset. While you pause, they're earning rankings, reviews, and brand recall that get harder to overcome the longer you wait.

  • You cap your revenue at your referral network

    Word-of-mouth is great — until it plateaus. Without marketing, your growth ceiling is your current customers' address books. New customers require new reach.

  • Rebuilding costs more than staying consistent

    A dormant brand loses rankings, followers, and top-of-mind awareness. Starting over after a gap is almost always more expensive than maintaining momentum. Marketing pauses rarely pay for themselves.

  • Your brand gets perceived as smaller than it is

    Thin digital presence signals instability to prospects doing research. Even a business with great service loses deals to a competitor that simply looks more polished and active online.

The silver lining:

If you're investing anywhere close to the guideline, you're already well ahead of most competitors. The question isn't whether to invest — it's how much, and where. That's exactly what the rest of this guide answers.

How to Justify Your Marketing Spend

Knowing your budget is one thing. Knowing whether a specific campaign will actually make you money is another. Here's the math that successful businesses use to make that call.

The problem with using net profit margin to plan campaigns

Most small business owners think: "My profit margin is 10%, so I need $100,000 in new sales to justify a $10,000 ad campaign." That math is misleading — and it makes marketing look far harder to justify than it actually is.

The right tool for evaluating a marketing investment is your Contribution Margin — the revenue left over after paying only the variable costs tied directly to delivering that product or service (materials, labor, shipping, commissions). Your fixed costs — rent, salaries, insurance — are already being paid whether you run a campaign or not. Marketing needs to cover itself, not the whole business.

The Formula

1

Contribution Margin per Unit

Sale Price − Variable Cost = Contribution Margin
2

Break-Even Units

Campaign Budget ÷ Contribution Margin = Units to Break Even
3

Every sale after break-even = pure profit

Once the campaign pays for itself, the contribution margin from each additional unit drops straight to your bottom line. That's the real profit engine of marketing.

A worked example

Sale Price

$50

Variable Cost

$30

Campaign Budget

$10,000

Contribution margin: $50 − $30 = $20 per unit (40% of every sale covers fixed costs)

Naive 10% net margin rule says: you'd need $100,000 in sales to justify the campaign. That sounds hard.

Contribution margin reality: $10,000 ÷ $20 = 500 units → $25,000 in sales to break even. That's it.

Unit 501 and beyond: The campaign is paid for. Every additional sale contributes the full $20 directly to profit.

The long-game metric: CAC vs. LTV

Break-even analysis tells you whether a campaign pays for itself on the first sale. But most businesses grow on the value of customers over time — not just the first transaction. That's where Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) come in.

CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost

Total marketing & sales spend divided by the number of new customers gained. If you spent $5,000 this month and won 25 customers, your CAC is $200.

LTV — Customer Lifetime Value

Total gross margin a customer generates across their entire relationship with you. A customer who buys $300/year for 3 years at 40% margin has an LTV of $360.

The 3:1 Rule

Industry standard: your LTV should be at least 3× your CAC. If acquiring a customer costs $200 and they're worth $600 over their lifetime, you have a healthy, scalable business. Below 3:1, you're likely losing money on growth. Above 3:1, marketing spend is mathematically justified — and increasing it will increase profit.

5 Ways Small Businesses Waste Their Budget

It's not just about how much you spend. It's how you spend it. These five traps swallow more marketing dollars than almost anything else.

Many small businesses find one channel that works — say, Facebook ads — and pour everything into it. When that platform changes its algorithm or raises ad prices, the whole pipeline stalls. A healthy marketing mix uses at least three channels so no single change can wipe out your new-customer flow.
This is counterintuitive but backed by decades of research: businesses that maintain or increase marketing spend during slow periods consistently outperform competitors who cut. When competitors go quiet, you have less noise to cut through and often lower ad costs. Slow seasons are your best time to invest.
Paid ads drive traffic. Your website converts it. A slow, outdated, or hard-to-navigate site means you're paying to send people somewhere that doesn't work. Fix the website first — always. A modest site overhaul almost always delivers higher ROI than the same dollars sent in ads to a broken destination.
You don't need a complex analytics setup — but you do need to know which channels produce leads. Even asking "How did you hear about us?" tells you what's working. Without measurement, you're guessing. With it, you can shift dollars from what isn't working to what is. Track at minimum: lead source, cost per lead, and close rate by channel.
Acquiring a new customer costs 5–7× more than keeping an existing one. Yet most small business marketing budgets are 90% focused on acquisition. Email marketing, loyalty programs, and post-purchase follow-up are among the highest-ROI investments available — and they're almost always dramatically under-funded.

What is AEO — and why does it matter for your budget?

AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) is the practice of structuring your content so AI engines like ChatGPT, Google's AI Overviews, and Perplexity cite you as a source. GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) takes it further — optimizing to appear in AI-generated summaries and knowledge panels. Where SEO chased rankings, AEO/GEO chases citations. The investment is different. The payoff is different. And the window to get in early is closing.

A word about Performance Max

Google's automated Performance Max campaigns promise simplicity, but many small businesses report wasted spend on irrelevant placements with minimal transparency into where the budget actually goes. If you're running PMax, audit your placement reports regularly — or consider sticking with manually controlled Search and Display campaigns where you have more control.

How should this shift your budget allocations?

The old SEO playbook was about keyword density and backlinks. The new playbook is about being the best answer in your niche — clearly, concisely, and in formats AI can parse and cite (FAQ schema, structured headers, direct definitions).

Budget implication: Redirect a portion of your SEO retainer or agency budget toward content that's built to answer specific questions. Think "what is," "how to," and "best [X] for [Y]" structured content — not just long-form blog posts chasing keywords.

Rising CPCs don't mean you should abandon Google Ads — they mean you need to be more surgical. Focus paid budget on high-intent, bottom-of-funnel keywords where the cost per acquisition still pencils out. Use exact-match and phrase-match keywords, not broad.

Budget implication: If your average transaction value is under $200, rising CPCs likely make standalone Google Ads unprofitable without extraordinary conversion rates. Supplement with organic and social rather than throwing more budget at paid search hoping it scales.

AI engines cite sources they trust. Trust is built through consistent, original, expert content published under a real byline — reviews, local citations, PR mentions, and third-party links that establish your business as the authority in your niche.

Budget implication: This is where PR, podcast appearances, local media, and community partnerships pay off in a new way. A mention in a local news story or an industry publication isn't just good for brand awareness — it now signals authority to AI models that determine whose business gets cited.

With 40% of Gen Z using social platforms as search engines, your discoverability can no longer depend on Google alone. YouTube, TikTok, Instagram Reels, and even Pinterest have their own search ecosystems — and in some product categories, they outperform Google for purchase intent.

Budget implication: If your audience skews under 35, consider reallocating 10–15% of your Google-centric spend toward short-form video content and platform-native SEO (hashtags, captions, alt text, and engagement signals that feed platform algorithms).
